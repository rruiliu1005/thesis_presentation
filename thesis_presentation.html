Topic 2: Brief Introduction to Copulas

Copulas

“A function which joins or ‘couples’ multivariate distribution function to its one-dimensional marginal distribution functions.” - Roger Nelson

Let \(F \in \mathfrak{F}(F_1, ..., F_d)\) be a d-multivariate distribution, and \(F_j\) be the \(j\)th univariate margin for \(j = 1,...,d\).

The copula associated with \(F\) is a distribution function \(C:[0,1]^d \rightarrow [0,1]\) such that:

\[F(x) = C(F_1(x_1), ..., F_d(x_d)) = C(u_1,..., u_d)) \qquad \text{where} \qquad x\in \mathbb{R}^d \qquad\] Let \[X_1,...,X_d \sim F_1,...,F_d\] then \[\qquad F_j(X_j) \quad = \quad U_j \qquad \text{are uniformly distributed} \qquad \text{for} \quad j = 1,...,d \quad \] and \[U_1,...,U_d \sim C\] Moreover, if \(F\) is a continuous \(d\)-variate distribution function with univariate margins \(F_1,...,F_d\), and quantile functions \(F^{-1}_1,...,F^{-1}_d\), then \[C(u) \quad = \quad F(F^{-1}_1(u_1),...,F^{-1}_d(u_d)), \qquad u\in [0,1]^d\] is the unique copula of \(F\).

Common Copulas

Copula contour with Kendall’s \(\tau = 0.7\)

Gaussian Copula The multivariate Gaussian copula cdf is: \[C_{\Sigma}(u,\Sigma) \quad = \quad \Phi_d(\Phi^{-1}(u_1),...\Phi^{-1}(u_d); \Sigma)\] where

- \(\Phi_d(\cdot)\) is the multivariate Gaussian distribution CDF

t-Copula The multivariate t-copula cdf is: \[C_{\nu, \Sigma}(u_1,...,u_d) = T_{\nu,\Sigma}(T^{-1}_\nu(u_1),...T^{-1}_\nu(u_d))\] where - \(T_{\nu,\Sigma}(\cdot)\) is the multivariate t-distribution CDF

Frank Copula The multivariate Frank copula cdf is: \[C(u_1, u_2,...,u_d; \theta) = -\frac{1}{\theta}ln\left(1+\frac{\prod_{i=1}^d(1-e^{-\theta u_i})}{(e^{-\theta}-1)}\right)\] where - \(\theta \in \mathbb{R}\) is the copula parameter which determines the strength and type of dependence

Gumbel Copula The multivariate Gumbel copula cdf is: \[C_\theta(u_1, u_2, ..., u_d) \quad = \quad exp\left(-\left[\sum_{i=1}^d(-ln \; u_i)^\theta\right]^{1/\theta}\right)\] where - \(\theta > 1\) is the dependence strength parameter

Reflected Gumbel Copula The multivariate Reflected Gumbel copula cdf is: \[C_\theta(u_1, u_2, ..., u_d) \quad = \quad exp\left(-\left[\sum_{i=1}^d(-ln (1-u_i))^\theta\right]^{1/\theta}\right)\] where - \(\theta\) is the dependence strength parameter \(\theta > 1\)

Clayton Copula The multivariate Clayton copula cdf is: \[C_\theta(u_1,...,u_d) \quad = \quad \left( \sum_{i=1}^{d}u_i^{-\theta} - d + 1 \right)^{-\frac{1}{\theta}}\] where - \(\theta \geq 0\) is the copula parameter that controls the degree of dependence between the variables

Factor Copulas

In the copula model with \(p\) factors, the transformed variables \[U_1,...,U_d = F_1(X_1),...,F_d(X_d)\] are regarded as independent conditional on \(p\) latent variables \(V_1,...,V_p\). Then we have: \[ C(u_1,...u_d) = \underbrace{\int_...\int}_\text{p}\prod_{k=1}^{d}F_{k|V_1,...V_p}(u_k|v_1,...v_p)dv_1...dv_p \] where \(F_{k|V_1,...V_p}(u_k|v_1,...v_p)\) can be expressed as a function of bi-variate copulas, conditional distribution of given latent variables .

Preliminary Steps

ARMA-GARCH Filtering \(ARMA(p,q)\)
Let
- \(y_t\) denote the time-series value at time \(t\)
- \(\epsilon_t\) denote the error term (white noise) at time \(t\) where \(E(\epsilon_t) = 0\) and \(Var(\epsilon_t) = \sigma^2\)
Then: \[y_t = \mu + \phi_1y_{t-1} + \phi_2y_{t-2} + \cdots + \rho_p y_{t-p} + \epsilon_t\] where

- \(p\) is the order of the AR component
- \(q\) is the order of the MA component
- \(\epsilon_t\) is the error term at time \(t\), assumed to follow a white noise process with zero mean and constant variance

\(GARCH(p,q)\)
Let
- \(\sigma_t\) denote error variance term at time \(t\)
Then: \[\sigma^2_t = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 y^2_{t-1} + ... + \alpha_p y^2_{t-p} + \beta_1 \sigma^2_{t-1} + ... + \beta_q \sigma^2_{t-q}\] where

- \(\alpha_{0} > 0\) and \(\alpha_i \geq 0\) for \(i = 1, \dots, p\)
- \(\beta_0 > 0\) and \(\beta_i \geq 0\) for \(i = 1,....,q\)

Ljung-Box Test:
\(H_0\):There are no autocorrelations in the series
\(H_1\): There are autocorrelations present in the series

Model 1 - Standard One Factor Copula Model

Model Setup In a one factor copula, we assume our variables of interest \[X_{i1},...,X_{id} \in \mathbb{R} \qquad \text{ for } i = 1,...,n\] have been transformed to uniform random variables using their respective univariate marginal distributions \[U_{i1},...,U_{id} \sim Uniform(0,1) \qquad \text{ for } i = 1,...,n \] We further assume that \(U_{i1},...,U_{id}\) are conditionally independent given the latent variable \(V_1\), and we assume \(V_1 \sim Uniform(0,1)\)
Then, let \(F_{U_j|V_1}\) denote the conditional cumulative distribution function of \(U_j\) given \(V_1\), we have: \[ F_{X_1,...,X_d}(x_{i1},...,x_{id}) = C(u_{i1},...,u_{id}) = \int_0^1 \prod_{j=1}^{d}F_{U_j|V_1}(u_{ij}|v_{1})dv_1 = \int_0^1 \prod_{j=1}^{d}C_{U_j|V_1}(u_{ij}|v_{1})dv_1 \]

Since \(U_j\) and \(V_1\) are \(Uniform(0,1)\) random variables, and \[F_{U_j|V_1} (u_{ij}|v_{1}) = \frac{\partial }{\partial V_1}C_{U_j,V_1}(u_j,v_1) = C_{U_j|V_1}(u_j|v_1)\] Therefore,
 \[c(u_{i1},...,u_{id}) = \frac{\partial^d}{\partial u_{i1},...,u_{id}}C(u_{i1},...,u_{id}) = \int_0^1\prod_{j=1}^d c_{U_j, V_1}(u_{ij},v_{1})dv_1\] since \[\frac{\partial}{\partial u_{ij}}C_{U_j|V_1}(u_{ij}|v_{1}) = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial U_j \partial V_1} C_{U_j, V_1}(u_{ij},v_{1}) = c_{U_j, V_1}(u_{ij},v_{1})\]

Model 1 - Standard One Factor Copula Model

Model Results The tail weighted measure of dependence is defined as: \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:tail_weighted_measure_of_dependence} \rho_L(a,p) = Cor\left( a\left(1 - \frac{1-U_1}{p}\right), a\left(1 - \frac{1-U_2}{p}\right) \middle| U_1 < p, U_2 < p \right) \end{equation}\] \[\rho_U(a,p)= Cor\left ( a\left(1 - \frac{1-U_1}{p}\right), a\left(1 - \frac{1-U_2}{p}\right) \middle| 1-U_1 < p, 1- U_2 < p\right)\] where
- \(\rho_U\) and \(\rho_L\) are the upper and lower tail-weighted dependence measures
- \(a(\cdot)\) : \([0,1]\rightarrow (0,\infty)\) is a continuous function
- \(p\) is the truncation level satisfying \(0<p\leq 0.5\)

We take \(a\) to be the power function \(a(u) = u^k\) and define squared errors for the tail-weighted measure of dependence as follows:

\[ \text{SE Upper Tail} = \sum_i^d\sum_j^d(\rho_{U; i,j}^{\text{simulated}} - \rho_{U; i,j}^{\text{observed}})^2 \\ \text{SE Lower Tail} = \sum_i^d\sum_j^d(\rho_{L; i,j}^{\text{simulated}} - \rho_{L; i,j}^{\text{observed}})^2 \]

Model 2 - Dynamic Dependence Structure

Model Setup Non-parametric, dynamic modelling of the dependence parameters.

Find the maximum likelihood estimators \(\widehat{\theta}_{jTd_m}\) for \(j = 1,..,p\) and \(d_m \in {T,...,N}\) \[L(\mathbf{u_1},...,\mathbf{u_d}; \theta_{1Td_m},..., \theta_{dTd_m}) = \prod_{i=d_m-T+1}^{d_m} \int_0^1\prod_{j=1}^d c_{U_j, V_1}(u_{ij},v_{1}; \theta_{jTd_m})dv_{1}\] T is the rolling-window size.
By minimizing the negative of the log likelihood below: \[l(\mathbf{u_1},..,\mathbf{u_d}; \theta_{1Td_m},..., \theta_{dTd_m}) = \sum_{i=d_m-T+1}^{d_m} log\left(\int_0^1\prod_{j=1}^dc_{U_{j}, V_{1}}(u_{ij}, v_{1}; \theta_{jTd_m})dv_{1}\right)\]

Topic 4: Computational Details

Computational Complexity of the One Factor Copula Model

The likelihood function for the one factor copula model with \(n\) observations and \(d\) variables is defined as:

\[L(\mathbf{u_1},...,\mathbf{u_d}; \theta_1,...\theta_d) = \prod_{i=1}^n \int_0^1\prod_{j=1}^d c_{U_j, V_1}(u_{ij},v_1; \theta_j)dv_{1}\]

Computing the likelihood for a factor copula model can be computationally expensive for several reasons
  • For each observation each variable, we need to compute the values of all the bi-variate copula density
  • The product of the \(d\) bivariate copula densities must be integrated out with respect to the latent variable using the Gauss-Legendre method
    e.g. for each observation \(i\) and \(m\) number of quadrature points, we need to compute \(m \times d\) number of bi-variate copula densities
  • The likelihood function needs to be evaluated multiple times to find the optimal value

e.g. with 2,000 observations, 20 variables and 35 quadrenture points, we need to compute 1.4 million bivariate copula densities; if the optimization algorithm converges in 100 iterations, the total number of calculations becomes 140 million Furthermore, the derivative of the log likelihood function with respect to a parameter \(\theta_k\) can be derived as: \[\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta_k}l(\mathbf{u_1},..,\mathbf{u_d}; \theta_1,..., \theta_d) = \sum_{i=1}^n \left\{\frac{\int_0^1\prod_{j\neq k}c_{U_j,V_{1}}(u_{ij}, v_{1}, \theta_j) \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta_k}c_{u_k, v_1}(u_{ik}, v_{1}; \theta_k)dv_{1}}{\int_0^1\prod_{j = 1}^d c_{U_j,V_{1}} (u_{ij}, v_{1}, \theta_j)} \right\} \] For each iteration of the optimization process, we must compute \(n \times d \times m\) bi-variate copula values and their corresponding derivatives for each model parameter, further compounding the computational cost.

Optimization Strategies

  • Use of Gauss-Legendre Method for Numerical Integration
  • Extensive Use of Matrix Operations with Efficient Packages
  • Efficient Product Calculation Using apply(), mapply() and do.call()
  • Mathematical Techniques
  • Efficient Handling of Gradients
  • Reuse of Intermediate Calculations
  • Use of Parallel Processing
  • Use of Rcpp for C++ Functions

Optimization Strategies (Cont’d)

  • Use of Gauss-Legendre Method for Numerical Integration
    • In the Gauss-Legendre method, the integral of a function \(f(x)\) over the interval \([-1,1]\) with \(\mathbf{m}\) number of quadrature points is approximated as: \[\int_{-1}^{1}f(x)dx \approx \sum_{k=1}^{m}w_kf(x_k)\]
  • Extensive Use of Matrix Operations
    • For example, to perform the numerical integration in both the likelihood and the derivative functions, we define a matrix \(V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times m}\) \[V = \begin{bmatrix} v_{11} & v_{12} & \cdots & v_{1m} \\ v_{21} & v_{22} & \cdots & v_{2m} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ v_{d1} & v_{d2} & \cdots & v_{dm} \end{bmatrix} \]
      • Gauss-Legendre quadrature points are replicated across observations
      • Elliminate the need to recalculate these values for every loop iteration, leveraging vectorized computations
      • The integral is also recycled to calculate both the likelihood function value as well as the derivative

Optimization Strategies (Cont’d)

  • Mathematical Techniques
    • Gaussian One Factor Copula: the \(j \neq k\) term cancels
        \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \rho_k}l(\mathbf{u_1},..,\mathbf{u_d}; \rho_1,..., \rho_d) = \sum_{i=1}^n \left\{\frac{\int_0^1\prod_{j=1}^dc_{U_j,V_{1}}(u_{ij}, v_{1}, \rho_j) \frac{\rho_k(1-\rho_k^2) + \Phi^{-1}(u_{ik})\Phi^{-1}(v_{1})(1+\rho_k^2) - \rho_k(\Phi^{-1}(u_{ik})^2 + \Phi^{-1}(v_{1})^2)}{(1-\rho_k^2)^2}dv_{1}}{\int_0^1\prod_{j=1}^dc_{U_k,V_{1}}(u_{ik}, v_{1}, \rho_k) dv_{1}} \right\} \]

    • Gumbel and Clayton One Factor Copula
      Problem: The \(j \neq k\) term does not cancel
      Solution:

      \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} c_{U_1,V_{1}}(u_{i1}, v_{1}) & c_{U_2,V_{1}}(u_{i2}, v_{1}) & \cdots & c_{U_d,V_{1}}(u_{id}, v_{1}) \\ c_{U_1,V_{1}}(u_{i1}, v_{1}) & c_{U_2,V_{1}}(u_{i2}, v_{1}) & \cdots & c_{U_d,V_{1}}(u_{id}, v_{1}) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ c_{U_1,V_{1}}(u_{i1}, v_{1}) & c_{U_2,V_{1}}(u_{i2}, v_{1}) & \cdots & c_{U_d,V_{1}}(u_{id}, v_{1}) \end{bmatrix} \qquad B = \begin{bmatrix} c_{U_1,V_{1}}(u_{i1}, v_{1}) & c_{U_2,V_{1}}(u_{i2}, v_{1}) & \cdots & c_{U_d,V_{1}}(u_{id}, v_{1}) \end{bmatrix} \] so that when we compute the column products of the matrix A, the matrix becomes: \[\begin{align*} A_{prod}= \begin{bmatrix} \prod_{j=1}^dc_{U_k,V_{1}}(u_{ik}, v_{1}, \theta_k) & \prod_{j=1}^dc_{U_k,V_{1}}(u_{ik}, v_{1}, \theta_k) & \cdots & \prod_{j=1}^dc_{U_k,V_{1}}(u_{ik}, v_{1}, \theta_k) \\ \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}\] Finally, dividing by the row vector B, we have: \[\begin{align*} \frac{A_{prod}}{B} = \begin{bmatrix} \prod_{j \neq 1}c_{U_k,V_{1}}(u_{ik}, v_{1}, \theta_k) & \prod_{j \neq 2}c_{U_k,V_{1}}(u_{ik}, v_{1}, \theta_k) & \cdots & \prod_{j \neq d}c_{U_k,V_{1}}(u_{ik}, v_{1}, \theta_k) \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ \end{align*}\]
